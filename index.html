The Archive

Portfolio Abstract

This archive documents a shift in my understanding of writing and computer science. When I started this course, I wasn't quite sure what even counted as "writing" within computer science. I mostly viewed writing as a wrapper or way of expressing the coding and problem-solving process. Through two projects as well as journal entries and discussion posts, I have come to see that writing is what does computer science. This archive traces my shift from the "gold star" mindset described by my instructor I interviewed, where writing in classrooms is done mostly for the purpose of a grade or instructor approval, to a better understanding that writing enacts disciplinarity, mediates activity, and constructs professional identity.

This site itself is a showcase of the "a multimodal stack" framework I now view computer science through. The HTML and CSS structure beneath this page was created through writing, as well as the git commits and text included, even though no other people were directly involved with the creation.

Synthesis: Projects 1 & 2

In project 1, I focused on exploring the values of CS writing (precision, transparency, working products) while in project 2, I zoomed in on a specific discourse community, Scrum, to see how those values translate to practice.

One of my major realizations was that the ways of communicating in Scrum are via specific genres that each serve rhetorical purposes. They structure collaboration, distribute authority, and gate participation. Writing is the mechanism through which work in Scrum happens.

Many of the ideas discussed within project 1 ("Your writing defines you") came up again as I completed project 2. Within scrum, your role on the team defines which writing you are expected to do, and the writing you do reinforces your position on the team.

My project 2 draft was stuck in what Johnson & Krase referred to as the "paper-for-grade economy" that the FYC participants were in, described in their paper, "Coming to learn: From First-Year Composition to Writing in the Disciplines." You can see that my project 2 closely follows the rubric's structure without making the meaning that meant enough to me. I added a significant amount between project 2 and the final, all highlighted in green within the final PDF. I realized after turning in my project 2 draft, that I was still using the "gold star" approach.

This archive also serves as an even further revision of my project 2, recontextualized within my whole semester's body of work.

Looking Forward

Writing mediates activity. In CS, writing is not just a way to note and share information, it is the mechanism that actually does the work.

  • Code mediates the interactions between the programmer and machine.
  • Documentation mediates between developers and the team.
  • Research papers mediate between scientists and the community.

A breakdown in writing at any layer can cascade through the whole system, making writing one of the most important skills for any emerging computer scientist.

These concepts give me a useful tool box to take into my professional life. When working in the industry, I will now understand the documentation I am writing is not just to make my code nicer or more easily understandable, it is mediating activity between the rest of the development team and myself. Mastery of computer science requires mastery of its writing, because the writing does the computer science.

Design

For the design of this website, I chose to write it from scratch using HTML (Hypertext Markup Language) for the body and text as well as CSS, or Cascading Style Sheets to set the look and style. HTML is the standard for websites, and when you use an online website editing tool, you are likely also editing HTML, just through a layer of abstraction and a nice user interface. By writing the website from scratch, it gives me full control of how my website looks and feels as well as allowing me to easily host the page on github for free and further developing my webdesign skills. I will likely have to work with HTML and CSS frequently throughout my journey as a computer scientist.

I chose this layout to keep the site simple and linear. All it takes to navigate is scrolling down, but I have also provided a bar at the top to easily navigate to different sections.

I chose the windows XP default wallpaper, known as Bliss, not only because it look pleasant, but it also is estimated to be seen by billions of people and most likely by anyone who is viewing this website, while also reminding the audience of computers, as the image is burned into the mind of many computer scientists who grew up on windows.
